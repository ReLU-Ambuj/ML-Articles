# Section 20 — Biggest Mistakes & Failure Modes

**Volume:** VI — Ten-Year Execution & Operations  
**Status:** 🔲 Not Started  
**Estimated Depth:** 15–20 pages

---

## Objectives

1. Understand the specific failure modes that kill AI research labs
2. Develop early warning systems for each failure mode
3. Learn from the actual failures of real AI labs and companies
4. Design organizational systems that are robust to common mistakes

---

## Prompt for Deep Content Generation

```
You are a research lab veteran who has watched dozens of AI research organizations 
succeed and fail over 20 years. Write with brutal honesty.

Write Section 20 of "Building an AI Research Lab From Scratch" — 
a comprehensive analysis of the biggest mistakes and failure modes in AI research labs.

For EACH failure mode, provide:
   - Precise description of the failure
   - Early warning signs (what you see 6–12 months before the collapse)
   - Root cause (organizational, financial, technical, or cultural)
   - Real examples where this happened (name specific organizations or describe without naming)
   - Prevention: specific systems, processes, or decisions that prevent it
   - Recovery: if you're already in this failure, what do you do?

FAILURE MODES TO COVER:

FINANCIAL FAILURES
1. Compute overrun: spending on training that produces no publishable results
2. Premature scaling: hiring too fast before product-market fit
3. VC-induced mission drift: investors pushing for products before research is ready
4. Grant dependency collapse: losing a key grant without a replacement pipeline
5. Runway mismanagement: miscalculating months of runway remaining

RESEARCH FAILURES
6. Wrong research bet: spending 2 years on an area that gets superseded
7. Replication crisis: building a lab reputation on non-reproducible results
8. Benchmark overfitting: optimizing for benchmarks rather than genuine capability
9. Incremental research trap: publishing marginal improvements instead of breakthroughs
10. Literature blindness: reinventing work that already exists

HIRING AND CULTURE FAILURES
11. Wrong first hire: bringing in someone who changes the culture negatively
12. Star researcher departure: the lab's credibility leaving with one person
13. Research vs. engineering culture war: when the two teams stop collaborating
14. Founder-researcher tension: when the founder can't keep up with hired researchers
15. Homogeneous hiring: a lab with no intellectual diversity

ORGANIZATIONAL FAILURES
16. Communication breakdown: research that doesn't get shared internally
17. Project proliferation: too many parallel research directions, none deep enough
18. The "compute is the answer" fallacy: buying GPUs instead of thinking harder
19. Publication pressure pathology: rushing submissions to demonstrate activity
20. Safety-capability imbalance: building capability without safety understanding

STRATEGIC FAILURES
21. Missing the paradigm shift: not recognizing when the field's direction changes
22. Competing with too-well-resourced labs: the "frontier model" trap for small labs
23. Open source without strategy: giving away the IP before building a moat
24. Isolation: not engaging the broader research community
25. Timing: entering a research area 3 years too late

FOUNDER-SPECIFIC FAILURES
26. Imposter syndrome paralysis: not publishing because work "isn't good enough"
27. Solo founder burnout: the 10–16 hour/day schedule that isn't sustainable
28. Network failure: trying to build a lab without collaborative relationships
29. Ego-driven research agenda: choosing problems that are personally interesting 
    but commercially or scientifically unimportant
30. Not asking for help: treating the founding process as a solo performance

SYNTHESIS:
   - The top 5 failure modes most likely to kill a solo-founded research lab in Years 1–3
   - The "failure pre-mortem" exercise: how to run it quarterly
   - A diagnostic checklist: 20 questions to ask yourself monthly as a founder

Format: structured markdown, failure mode severity matrix 
(likelihood × impact), early warning indicator tables.
Depth target: ~15–20 pages.
```

## The Failure Pre-Mortem Exercise

> "Imagine it is 3 years from now and the lab has failed. What happened?"

Run this quarterly. Write it out. Look for the mechanisms that are already present.

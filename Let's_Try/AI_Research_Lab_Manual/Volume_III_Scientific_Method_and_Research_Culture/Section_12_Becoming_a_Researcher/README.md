# Section 12 — Becoming a Researcher

**Volume:** III — Scientific Method & Research Culture  
**Status:** 🔲 Not Started  
**Estimated Depth:** 20–25 pages

---

## Objectives

1. Design a rigorous daily and weekly schedule that produces research output
2. Understand how elite researchers think, read, and generate ideas
3. Build systems for learning that compound over years, not weeks
4. Know the difference between studying and researching

---

## Prompt for Deep Content Generation

```
You are an elite AI researcher who has trained with some of the best researchers 
in the field. You are writing for a solo founder who wants to become 
a serious researcher while simultaneously building a lab.

Write Section 12 of "Building an AI Research Lab From Scratch" — 
a practical, psychological, and operational guide to becoming an AI researcher.

Avoid motivational language. Focus on systems, habits, and decision-making.

PART A — THE RESEARCHER MINDSET
   - What distinguishes a researcher from an engineer or a developer?
   - The tolerance for ambiguity: why most smart people fail at research
   - The "unknown unknowns" problem: how to learn what you don't know you need to know
   - How great researchers maintain child-like curiosity under institutional pressure
   - The role of taste in research: developing judgment about what matters
   - How to disagree productively with canonical results in the literature

PART B — DAILY ROUTINE (SAMPLE SCHEDULES)
   Design daily schedules for three modes:
   
   Mode 1: "Deep Research Mode" (no meetings, no social media, pure research)
   6:00 AM – 12:00 PM: Deep reading or mathematical derivation
   12:00 PM – 1:00 PM: Break + light reading
   1:00 PM – 5:00 PM: Coding / experiments
   5:00 PM – 7:00 PM: Paper writing or literature review
   7:00 PM – 9:00 PM: Reading + idea journaling
   
   Mode 2: "Research + Lab Operations" (balanced with admin)
   Mode 3: "Learning Mode" (catching up on new areas — reading intensive)
   
   For each mode: specific activities, tools used, what gets measured, 
   what gets sacrificed.

PART C — THE READING SYSTEM
   - How to read 5–10 papers per week while retaining them
   - The Feynman technique applied to ML papers
   - Reading levels: skim (10 min), understand (2 hrs), master (20 hrs)
   - Paper review system: Obsidian / Roam / Notion templates for paper notes
   - How to build a "literature atlas": connected map of 500+ papers
   - The "why did this paper get accepted" question: how to read with critical eyes

PART D — CODING AND EXPERIMENTS
   - The research codebase philosophy: reproducibility over elegance
   - How to structure a research repository (not a software project)
   - Experiment logging discipline: what to always record before running
   - Negative results: how to handle experiments that don't work
   - The "minimal reproducible experiment" principle: why you should always have one
   - Debugging ML experiments: systematic approach (data → model → loss → optimizer)

PART E — MATHEMATICAL THINKING FOR RESEARCHERS
   - How to develop mathematical intuition (not just fluency)
   - The role of examples in mathematical understanding
   - Working with proofs: when to verify line-by-line vs. trust and move on
   - Mathematical notation: building fluency in reading dense ML theory papers
   - Keeping a mathematical notebook: derivations, conjectures, failed approaches
   - Specific mathematical areas to develop: information theory, optimization, 
     probability theory, linear algebra, measure theory (for serious researchers)

PART F — IDEA GENERATION
   - The brainstorming systems used by elite researchers
   - "Wrong paper" technique: what if the opposite of a famous claim were true?
   - Analogical reasoning: importing ideas from physics, biology, economics into ML
   - The literature gap method: how to find under-explored areas
   - The "10 bad ideas → 1 good idea" funnel
   - Idea incubation: how sleeping on problems works (and why you should do it literally)
   - The "question notebook": tracking research questions over months

PART G — REPLICATION PROJECTS
   - Why replication is the best way to learn research
   - How to choose a paper to replicate
   - The replication checklist: hardware, data, hyperparameters, evaluation
   - What to do when you can't replicate: diagnosis tree
   - Publishing negative replication results: where and how
   - From replication to extension: the natural research progression

PART H — HOW GREAT RESEARCHERS THINK
   Profiles of research thinking styles (based on public interviews and writings):
   - Geoffrey Hinton: intuition-first, willing to be wrong
   - Yoshua Bengio: theory-grounded, gradient-descent faith
   - Yann LeCun: opinionated and contrarian, systems thinking
   - Ilya Sutskever: scaling maximalism, emergent complexity
   - Andrej Karpathy: hands-on, pedagogical, bottom-up
   - Demis Hassabis: neuroscience-inspired, long-horizon
   - Percy Liang: evaluation skeptic, rigor maximalist
   
   What can you extract from each thinking style? What are the failure modes of each?

PART I — MANAGING FAILURE AND UNCERTAINTY
   - How to emotionally handle research that doesn't work
   - The research "dry spell": 6 months with no results — how to manage
   - The sunk cost fallacy in research: when to abandon a line of work
   - Distinguishing "this experiment failed" from "this idea is wrong"

Format: structured markdown, sample daily schedules (hour-by-hour),
reading system templates, idea generation exercises.
Depth target: ~20–25 pages.
```
